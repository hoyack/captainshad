# Nearshore and Offshore Fish Species - Aransas Pass / Port Aransas / Rockport, TX
## Research Findings - Texas Gulf Coast Fishing Species Guide

---

## Notes on Data Sources
- Sources: Texas Parks & Wildlife Department (TPWD), NOAA Fisheries, Coastal Charters TX, CAT Sport Fishing, Fisherman's Wharf Port Aransas, Visit Port Aransas, Jawjackers Charters, local fishing guides
- Federal waters: 9-200 nautical miles from shore
- Texas state waters: 0-9 nautical miles from shore (open year-round for red snapper)
- Average water temps: Winter 61-67F, Spring 66-78F, Summer 82-86F, Fall 72-85F

---

## SNAPPER SPECIES

### 1. Red Snapper
- **Scientific Name:** *Lutjanus campechanus*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round in Texas state waters (0-9 nm); Federal season typically June-September (varies annually)
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August
- **Typical Locations:** Natural and artificial reefs in 60-100+ feet; offshore rigs, wrecks, bottom structure. Texas state waters (within 9 miles) offer year-round access.
- **Notes:** The most popular offshore species. Federal for-hire season typically opens June 1. State waters bag limit: 4 fish, 15" minimum. Federal waters: 2 fish, 16" minimum. Most limits caught within first few hours.

### 2. Mangrove (Gray) Snapper
- **Scientific Name:** *Lutjanus griseus*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round
- **Peak Months:** April through August (summer spawning season most active)
- **Typical Locations:** Jetties, pilings, bulkheads, rip-rap inshore; reefs, wrecks, platforms offshore at 60-120 feet
- **Notes:** Hard-fighting snapper with sharp teeth. Often called "black snapper" in Texas. Found around any structure. Average 18" offshore, with fish over 30" possible. No specific Texas state bag limit listed; federal: 10 fish aggregate snapper limit, 12" minimum.

### 3. Lane Snapper
- **Scientific Name:** *Lutjanus synagris*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round
- **Peak Months:** Spring through Fall (March-November)
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore reefs, wrecks, offshore structure
- **Notes:** Smaller snapper with distinctive yellow fins. No closed season. No bag limit in Texas. Often caught when red snapper season is closed. Minimum size 8" total length.

### 4. Vermilion Snapper (Beeliner)
- **Scientific Name:** *Rhomboplites aurorubens*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round
- **Peak Months:** Spring through Fall
- **Typical Locations:** Offshore reefs, wrecks, bottom structure in 60-200+ feet
- **Notes:** Commonly called "beeliners." Consistent action when red snapper is closed. No closed season. No bag limit in Texas. Minimum size 10" total length. Excellent table fare.

## MACKEREL SPECIES

### 5. King Mackerel (Kingfish)
- **Scientific Name:** *Scomberomorus cavalla*
- **Present/Catchable:** March through October (best); some year-round
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August (peak); also good in March-May
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore structure 5-20 miles out, current seams, shrimp boats, offshore rigs
- **Notes:** Available almost year-round. Average 10-20 lbs, Texas record 81.88 lbs. Congregate around shrimp boats in summer. Wire leaders mandatory due to sharp teeth.

### 6. Spanish Mackerel
- **Scientific Name:** *Scomberomorus maculatus*
- **Present/Catchable:** March through October
- **Peak Months:** May through September
- **Typical Locations:** Jetties, surf, nearshore reefs, closer to shore than kings
- **Notes:** Smaller than kings, average 2-4 lbs. Hit spoons, jigs, and small live baits aggressively. Often found around the jetties and nearshore reefs.

---

## COBIA

### 7. Cobia (Ling / Lemonfish)
- **Scientific Name:** *Rachycentron canadum*
- **Present/Catchable:** March through September
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August (best); March-May for nearshore
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore buoys, floating debris, weed lines, offshore platforms, often following rays and sea turtles; between jetties and first set of rigs
- **Notes:** Average 15-30 lbs, can exceed 100 lbs. Texas record 108.44 lbs. Strong fighters. Sight-casting to cruising fish is exciting. First warm fronts in March push them into nearshore waters.

---

## TARPON

### 8. Tarpon (Silver King)
- **Scientific Name:** *Megalops atlanticus*
- **Present/Catchable:** June through September
- **Peak Months:** July and August
- **Typical Locations:** Beachfront, first and second guts in 15-40 feet of water, jetties, surf, passes
- **Notes:** Catch-and-release ONLY in Texas. Fish regularly exceed 100 lbs. Texas record 229 lbs (2017). Spectacular jumpers. Targeted with live bait or large artificial lures. One of the most exciting nearshore opportunities.

---

## SHARKS

### 9. Sharks (Various Species)
- **Scientific Name:** Multiple species (see below)
- **Present/Catchable:** May through October (peak); some year-round
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August (best action around shrimp boats)
- **Typical Locations:** Offshore, nearshore, beachfront, around shrimp boats, jetties
- **Notes:** Legal retainable species include:
  - **Blacktip Shark** (*Carcharhinus limbatus*): 24" minimum, acrobatic jumpers
  - **Spinner Shark** (*Carcharhinus brevipinna*): 24" minimum, similar to blacktip
  - **Bonnethead Shark** (*Sphyrna tiburo*): 24" minimum
  - **Bull Shark** (*Carcharhinus leucas*): 64" minimum
  - **Hammerhead Shark** (*Sphyrna* spp.): 99" minimum
  - **Atlantic Sharpnose Shark** (*Rhizoprionodon terraenovae*): 24" minimum
  - **Tiger Shark** (*Galeocerdo cuvier*): 64" minimum
  - **Lemon Shark** (*Negaprion brevirostris*): 64" minimum
  - Many charter operations practice catch-and-release. Non-offset circle hooks required in state waters. Daily bag: 1 shark per person (2 possession).

## AMBERJACK

### 10. Greater Amberjack
- **Scientific Name:** *Seriola dumerili*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round (most common in warmer months)
- **Peak Months:** March through November (warmer months)
- **Typical Locations:** Oil rigs, wrecks, deep structure, reefs
- **Notes:** "Bruisers of the reef." Average 30-50 lbs. Texas record 121 lbs. Dive hard for structure. Federal recreational season currently closed November 1 through July 31 (check NOAA for updates). Heavy tackle essential.

---

## GROUPER

### 11. Grouper (Various Species)
- **Scientific Name:** Multiple species (see below)
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round; prime season April-November
- **Peak Months:** May through September (summer most abundant)
- **Typical Locations:** Deeper structure, wrecks, reefs, ledges in 80-120+ feet
- **Notes:** Key species include:
  - **Red Grouper** (*Epinephelus morio*): No federal closed season. Found on reefs/ledges.
  - **Gag Grouper** (*Mycteroperca microlepis*): Federal recreational closed Jan 1-August 31; opens September 1. 
  - **Black Grouper** (*Mycteroperca bonaci*): Deeper wrecks and reefs.
  - **Scamp Grouper** (*Mycteroperca phenax*): Common on offshore structure.
  - All groupers fight hard and immediately try to rock you into cover. Heavy tackle essential.

---

## TRIGGERFISH

### 12. Gray Triggerfish
- **Scientific Name:** *Balistes capriscus*
- **Present/Catchable:** March-May and August-December (federal closures Jan-Feb and Jun-Jul 31)
- **Peak Months:** March, April, May (before spawning closure); September, October, November
- **Typical Locations:** Reefs, wrecks, offshore structure, oil rigs
- **Notes:** Firm, sweet white meat. Federal recreational closed Jan 1-end of Feb and Jun 1-Jul 31. Size limit 15" fork length. Bag limit: 1 per person within 20 reef fish aggregate. Excellent table fare.

---

## MAHI-MAHI (DORADO)

### 13. Dorado (Mahi-Mahi / Dolphinfish)
- **Scientific Name:** *Coryphaena hippurus*
- **Present/Catchable:** May through September
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August
- **Typical Locations:** Weed lines, floating debris, temperature breaks in open water, typically 25-40+ miles offshore
- **Notes:** Most colorful fish in the Gulf. Average 5-15 lbs, bulls over 30 lbs. Aggressive feeders, spectacular jumpers. Excellent white meat. Look for clear blue water and surface debris.

---

## TUNA

### 14. Blackfin Tuna
- **Scientific Name:** *Thunnus atlanticus*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round; most consistent fall through winter
- **Peak Months:** October, November, December, January, February
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore and offshore oil rigs, deep-water structures, depths of 300-500 feet
- **Notes:** More common and closer to shore than yellowfin. Average 5-25 lbs. Night jigging around rigs is productive. Hit vertical jigs near the thermocline. Active even when water drops into low 60s.

### 15. Yellowfin Tuna
- **Scientific Name:** *Thunnus albacares*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round (no closed season); best July-September
- **Peak Months:** July, August, September (high season); also good fall/winter
- **Typical Locations:** Deep-water oil rigs, structures, 1,500+ foot depths, typically far offshore (60+ mile runs)
- **Notes:** Average 30-80 lbs, fish over 100 lbs possible. Texas record 216.2 lbs (1998). Strongest fighters pound-for-pound. Chunking, trolling, and vertical jigging all produce. Most active at night. Bag limit: 3 fish, 27" minimum.

---

## WAHOO

### 16. Wahoo
- **Scientific Name:** *Acanthocybium solandri*
- **Present/Catchable:** October through February (best); some year-round
- **Peak Months:** November, December, January (best concentrations)
- **Typical Locations:** 100-fathom curve, temperature breaks, 60-80 miles offshore
- **Notes:** One of the fastest fish in the ocean. Built like torpedoes with razor-sharp teeth. Texas record 124 lbs (1987). High-speed trolling with specialized lures is standard. Excellent firm white meat. Need calm weather for the long run.

---

## BILLFISH

### 17. Sailfish
- **Scientific Name:** *Istiophorus platypterus*
- **Present/Catchable:** June through September
- **Peak Months:** June, July, August
- **Typical Locations:** Deep water 30-50+ miles offshore, color breaks, temperature gradients
- **Notes:** Average 40-70 lbs. Most are released. Hit live bait and trolled ballyhoo. Require longer trips (10-12 hours). Part of the billfish trio with blue marlin. Bucket-list catch for serious anglers.

---

## BARRACUDA

### 18. Great Barracuda
- **Scientific Name:** *Sphyraena barracuda*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round (more common in warmer months)
- **Peak Months:** May through September
- **Typical Locations:** Offshore rigs, wrecks, reefs; occasionally jetties and nearshore structure in South Texas
- **Notes:** Aggressive predator. Most caught offshore in Texas waters but can frequent jetties in the border region. Average 16"-3+ feet. Great fun on topwater plugs, jigs. Caught on 56-hour trips at Fisherman's Wharf targeting tuna.

---

## TRIPLETAIL

### 19. Tripletail
- **Scientific Name:** *Lobotes surinamensis*
- **Present/Catchable:** April through early November
- **Peak Months:** May, June, July, August, September
- **Typical Locations:** Floating near buoys, crab trap floats, pilings, floating debris near surface
- **Notes:** Look like oversized leaves drifting in current. Excellent sight-casting opportunity. Average 5-15 lbs. White, flaky meat rivals snapper. Texas limit: 3 fish, 17" minimum. Migrate south for winter.

---

## JACK SPECIES

### 20. Jack Crevalle
- **Scientific Name:** *Caranx hippos*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round (strongest in warmer months)
- **Peak Months:** May, June, July, August (best); also good September-October
- **Typical Locations:** Bays, nearshore waters, surf, mouths of passes and rivers, around schools of baitfish
- **Notes:** Pure power, school in large numbers crashing bait pods. Average 10-20 lbs, can reach 50 lbs. Texas record 50.25 lbs. Not highly valued as food (bloody, strong-flavored); mostly catch-and-release. Excellent sport on light tackle. Peak abundance in summer when juveniles recruit to estuaries.

## ADDITIONAL NEARSHORE/OFFSHORE SPECIES

### 21. Blue Marlin
- **Scientific Name:** *Makaira nigricans*
- **Present/Catchable:** June through September
- **Peak Months:** July, August
- **Typical Locations:** Deep water 100+ miles offshore
- **Notes:** Can exceed 500 lbs. Most are released. Require long-range trips. Bucket-list species.

### 22. Pompano
- **Scientific Name:** *Trachinotus carolinus*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round (best spring and fall)
- **Peak Months:** March-May, September-November
- **Typical Locations:** Surf, nearshore waters, jetties
- **Notes:** Average 1-3 lbs. Excellent eating. Run the surf zone.

### 23. Bonito (Little Tunny)
- **Scientific Name:** *Euthynnus alletteratus*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round
- **Peak Months:** Summer months
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore and offshore, around structures
- **Notes:** Often used as bait for larger predators. Hit fast-moving jigs and spoons.

### 24. Spadefish
- **Scientific Name:** *Chaetodipterus faber*
- **Present/Catchable:** Year-round
- **Peak Months:** Spring through Fall
- **Typical Locations:** Nearshore reefs, wrecks, offshore structures
- **Notes:** Good eating, fun on light tackle. Often caught on shorter offshore trips.

## REGULATORY NOTES (as of 2025 - always check current)

- **Red Snapper State Waters (0-9 nm):** Year-round, 4 fish, 15" min
- **Red Snapper Federal Waters:** Season opens June 1 (for-hire through ~Sept 16; private longer)
- **Gray Triggerfish:** Closed Jan 1-end Feb AND Jun 1-Jul 31 federally
- **Greater Amberjack:** Federal recreational closed Nov 1-Jul 31
- **Gag Grouper:** Federal recreational closed Jan 1-Aug 31
- **Tarpon:** Catch-and-release ONLY in Texas
- **Sharks:** Circle hooks required; many species prohibited; check TPWD regulations
- **Descending devices/venting tools:** Required for reef fish in federal waters
